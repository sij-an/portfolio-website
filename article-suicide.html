Increasing Cases of Suicide

Jul 10, 2026 6 min read Sijan Upreti

Suicide is one of the most painful topics to talk about — and perhaps that is exactly why we need to talk about it more. Around the world, the numbers keep climbing quietly. According to the World Health Organization, more than 700,000 people die by suicide every year, and for each death, there are many more who attempt it. Behind every statistic is a real person, a real story, and a circle of people left asking "what could I have done?"

Why Are Cases Rising?

There is no single cause. Suicide is the result of a complex mix of factors — mental health struggles, financial stress, loneliness, bullying, academic pressure, relationship breakdowns, and sometimes, the sheer feeling that there is no way out. In recent years, a few trends have made things worse:

  • Social media pressure: The constant comparison, cyberbullying, and curated perfection on social platforms can make anyone feel inadequate or isolated, especially young people.
  • Academic and career stress: In many cultures, the weight of expectations from family and society creates unbearable pressure on students and young professionals.
  • Post-pandemic loneliness: COVID-19 severed social connections for millions. Even as the world reopened, the emotional damage lingered — anxiety, depression, and grief became widespread.
  • Stigma around mental health: In many communities, admitting you are struggling is still seen as weakness. This stops people from seeking help when they need it the most.

The Silent Struggle

One of the most heartbreaking things about suicide is how invisible the warning signs can be. People who are suffering often smile through their pain. They go to school, show up at work, laugh with friends — all while fighting a battle no one else can see. We have built a world where people feel safer hiding their pain than sharing it, and that is something we urgently need to change.

"The person who dies by suicide doesn't want to end their life — they want to end their pain."

What Can We Do?

We don't need to be therapists or counselors to make a difference. Sometimes, the simplest actions carry the most weight:

  • Listen without judgment. If someone opens up to you, don't rush to give advice. Just being heard can be healing.
  • Check in on people. A simple "how are you, really?" can open doors that a casual greeting never will.
  • Normalize seeking help. Talk openly about therapy, mental health days, and emotional well-being. Remove the shame around it.
  • Be kind online. Words on a screen carry real weight. What feels like a harmless comment to you could be devastating to someone already on the edge.
  • Know the resources. Familiarize yourself with helpline numbers and crisis support services in your area so you can share them when it matters.

A Note to Anyone Struggling

If you are reading this and you are going through something difficult — please know that your pain is valid, but it is not permanent. The darkness you feel right now does not define the rest of your life. There are people who care about you, even when it feels like no one does. Reaching out is not a sign of weakness — it is the bravest thing you can do.

You are not a burden. You matter. And the world is better with you in it.

If you or someone you know is in crisis, please reach out to a local helpline.
You are not alone.
